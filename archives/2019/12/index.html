Expedition Behaviour and Reverse Pyramids ▾

Tuesday, December 3rd 2019

  • Work

A Heads Up: this post is a hard-to-read jumble of thoughts. More a reference note to my future self, and/or a place where I can point friends to specific sections they may find helpful.’

“We understand life backwards but live it forwards” — Kierkegaard

For the past few months I’ve been thinking on how to best see leadership in startups…or more generally, leadership in any team situation where you can expect that ‘the going will get tough’.

During this time I…

…read through The Elephant in the Brain to try and understand how and why people rationalize different behaviors. Specifically, rationalizing self-serving behaviors (local) at the expense of group well-being (global).

…I read Adam Grant’s Give & Take after his podcast interview on The Knowledge Project hoping to learn how to better-recognize these behaviors.

…I read The Art of Strategy. It’s a Game Theory book, which I hoped would help teach me to better design cooperative systems. That is, systems which align self-interest and other-interest. Systems without conflict between the two.

I was interested to dig into the idea that these are not (necessarily) opposing ends of a spectrum. They are indepenent variables, and optimizing for one need not take away from the other.

“This hybrid engine of self-interest and concern for others serves a much wider circle of people than can be reached by self-interest or caring alone.” — Bill Gates

Systems which are well-designed for coorperation may keep a group moving in a mutually/other-beneficial direction even if bad-actors become involved.

…finally, I’ve been working through Seeking Wisdom. In keeping with the advice to read different books at different speeds (per their merit), this book has the one I think I’ve been reading most slowly, of any I’ve ever read.

Admist the very, very many other things in this book has a good section on the psychology of incentives.

…then, books aside, I also spent a good deal of time talking with friends, mentors, fellow-founders, and a CEO coach.

It’s been a deep dive on a single question.

My motivation in all of this was to: update my understanding and expectations of human self and other-oriented behaviors, with the practical purpose of designing better human systems and choosing better leaders.

“Be warned that if you wish, as I do, to build a society in which individuals cooperate generously and unselfishly towards a common good, you can expect little help from biological nature. Let us try to teach generosity and altruism, because we are born selfish.” — Richard Dawkins, The Selfish Gene

In the past I’ve made mistakes here — designing systems that led to conflicts in self vs other-oriented behaviors, choosing the wrong leaders, or both.

These mistakes hurt the team I led, and also equally hurt the leaders I shouldn’t have put in their roles.

When I asked for guidance from people (friends, mentors) outside our company, they would quickly categorize.

“We don’t like uncertainty or the unknown. We need to categorize, classify, organize, and structure the world. It simplifies life…Our brain is wired to perceive before it thinks - to use emotions before reason.” — Seeking Wisdom: From Darwin to Munger

They would either quickly categorize to ( 1 ) fit one of their own experiences (“Oh! This is exactly like when our company…”) which had little to do with our company. Or by ( 2 ) rapidly zooming-in on what they supposed to be a single, root cause.

“We tend to be looking for the root cause of something, but in complexity, there’s no root cause.” — Jennifer Garvey Berger, of Simple Habits for Complex Times

When I asked “Could our explanation of the event help us predict future similar events?” the answer with these observers’ root cause explanations was always a certain no. So I couldn’t put much stock in them…“Stories may be selected to prove something and may give us a delusional sense of clarity.”

Their root cause might’ve grazed around the edges of truth, but each root cause (and each person had decided on a different root cause) was too over-simplified to be practical. — Sidenote: 💙 Chris Argyris.

Given these people had only 2nd-hand information about our company, neither (1) nor (2) held much real learning for me. They (graciously) tried to help, but being on the outside looking in, they always had too little context to be effective.

I also tried asking the leaders who I had chosen, who didn’t work out — and they always had explanations ready for me. “It’s because this…, and it’s because that…”

In every case these explanations were always externally-focused: i.e. “You didn’t do…” and “You did”…or “The team didn’t do…” and “The Team did…” I never heard an explanation that incorporated the leaders’ own behaviors, own choices, or own responsibility.

Not surprisingly when the details of these externally-focused explanations from leaders who didn’t work out were shared, the other members of the team disagreed quite strongly.

Their explanations probably also grazed around the edges of truth, but in the end were more likely self-defensive than not, as …“[w]e deny and distort reality to feel more comfortable, especially when reality threatens our self-interest.”

There was something wrong, somewhere. Everyone seemed touching on some truth but never wholeformed, solid truths. It was a mess.

What’s worse, everyone’s advice changed over time. Before and after a leader (1) did or (2) did not work out, I would get completely different feedback.

Others would interview these potential leaders, alongside me. They would advocate for them, and judge them good leaders. Then, once failed, they would forget (or downplay) their earlier advocacy once reality was clear.

“‘Everything seems stupid when it fails.’ In hindsight, eveything seems obvious. But we should look at earlier decisions in the context of their own time…sometimes we have a hard time separating a true story from a false one. After an event a story is created so that the event makes sense.” — Seeking Wisdom

Anyway…

This was all a rambling way of saying that I couldn’t seem to learn much by simply polling those around me.

I wanted to actually get better: at (1) predicting potential leaders’ fit and abilities and (2) designing human systems for cooperation (≠ selfishness).

I also wanted to do so without leaning lazily into any kind of cynicism…

“We should be careful to get out of an experience only the wisdom in it, and stop there, lest we be like the cat that sits down on a hot stove-lid. She will not sit down on a hot stove-lid again — but also she will never sit down on a cold one anymore.” — Mark Twain

I hoped to maintain an optimistic, but perhaps more rationally-optimistic, view of human nature. I didn’t want the scars of bad experiences to pollute my thinking, but neither did I want to repeat those experiences.

So, the only answer was to do the work. To learn what seemed needed to stand behind cynicism-free, rational optimism about people, and leadership. To learn how to pick better leaders, and build better human systems.

tl;dr?

First, I believe in the past that I put too much emphasis on potential leaders’ words and too little emphasis on their past actions:

“Consider people’s actual accomplishments and past behavior over a long period of time rather than first impressions. Since people leave track records in life, an individual’s paper record if often predictive of future performance and behavior.” — Seeking Wisdom

I repeatedly chose leaders who said they wanted to be a leader, who said they wanted to face the challenges, etc. but who really didn’t have any ‘when the going gets tough’-type teamwork evidence in their past.

In hindsight that’s become obvious.

I would then support them for quite a long time, when they underperformed; these leaders told me as much at the time. But eventually, after months of supporting this I would lose faith and patience the leader would ever improve. Then, move into Setup to Fail Syndrome.

What I should’ve done is been more clear with myself about the situation. That I was supporting a leader, who shouldn’t have been there. That would’ve been better for everyone (including the leader).

But instead the lost-faith Setup to Fail send us on a downward spiral, in an already bad situation, that was worse for all.

…again, anyway…

What follows below is not a summary of what I learned. It’s just two isolated ideas that I want to write out, from the process.

Expedition Behavior

The first is a new mental model, Expedition Behavior. I believe this is (by far) the best mental model I’ve come across to describe the responsibilities, relationships, and personalities of good leadership in startups.

Most people say “it’s like a marriage”. But I think that saying “it’s like an expedition” is probably much closer to the truth.

The best summary of Expedition Behavior I’ve found is by Neil Schulman on The Clymb:

  • Support the Group’s Goals (i.e.; Group > Self-Interest)
  • Take Care of Yourself (i.e.; Mental and Phyiscal Health)
  • Pitch In (i.e.; Be Responsible, Do Your Work)
  • Help Others but Don’t Do Their Work For Them (i.e.; Over-Dependence)
  • Moderate Potentially Annoying Behaviors (i.e.; Self-evident)
  • Admit Shortcomings and Correct Them (i.e.; No Rationalizations)
  • Support Everyone’s Growth (i.e.; Build Everyone’s Skills)
  • Be “Cow-Like” (i.e.; Don’t Get Too Emotional)
  • Relaxed Awareness (i.e.; Pay Attention, Be Responsible)
  • Be Funny (i.e.; Help Each Other Enjoy It)

Expedition Behavior is about process skills, not technical skill. But it is technical skills that I/we tend to too heavily-emphasize when choosing leaders for difficult expeditions, or startups:

“Technical skills are often emphasized at the expense of group relations, particularly in expeditions when the goal is clear and a high level of skill is required. Technical skills, while not enough to ensure safety or goal attainment, are essential and usually determine one’s assignment on an expedition, such as lead climber or medical personnel. Interestingly, it is process skills which emerge as being critical to success as time proceeds and conditions change during a trip.” — Christine M. Cashel, EdD., Group dynamics: implications for successful expeditions

I did not ignore the need for leaders’ process skills — it’s need is self-evident.

However, I judged their process skills through conversations. I based process skills assesment on their words and my conversational-impression of their character, not by ‘actual accomplishments and past behavior over a long period of time’.

In hindsight, it’s clear that…

“…predicting who has “good” expedition behavior (or EB) can be hard. Expeditions have a way of bringing out both the best and the worst in people; and no matter how many questionnaires your team has filled out, how many pretrip meetings you’ve held, or how many years you’ve all known each other, sometimes things don’t turn out the way you expect them to.” — Dave Anderson and Molly Absolon, NOLS Expedition Planning

The Expedition Behaviour lens has been clarifying.

I would never appoint an expedition leader who was untested in practice. Just because they said they aspired to the role? Even if they showed technical-expertise as a great climbers in their local gym? Never.

Yet, that’s what I often did appointing leaders. I took an impression of their leadership, responsibility, determination, and other process skills in a conversation. Then I sent our expedition into the wild, with an untested leader at its head.

Small wonder it often went poorly.

It’s hard — a whole lot harder than those I’ve evaluated through conversations tend to predict…a 110% effort:

“…if everyone on an expedition felt as if he or she was doing 110 percent of the work, each was probably doing just enough. Expeditions require a lot of effort. There are logistical chores, long hard days moving from one camp to another, technical obstacles to overcome, camp tasks to perform, and sheer emotional endurance involved in going on a wilderness trip of any length of time.” — Dave Anderson and Molly Absolon, NOLS Expedition Planning

It’s when the ‘going gets tough’, when the weather turns, when the packs gets heavy, when leaders are most needed, when the untested leader is finally tested. Unfortunately it’s here, when it’s getting hard, that rationalizations often replace responsibility in untested leaders:

“Poor expedition behavior is a breakdown in human relations caused by selfishness, rationalization, ignorance of personal faults, dodging blame or responsibility, physical weakness, and in extreme cases, not being able to risk one’s own survival to insure that of a companion.” — Paul Petzoldt founder, National Outdoor Leadership School

In the future, I will verify process skills in practice rather than in conversation, with only technical skills being evidenced in a leader’s history.

I hope to do a better job myself, now that I (think) I better understand this.

Reverse Pyramids

The second idea is reverse pyramids.

Let’s start with normal pyramids — we’re all familiar with them.

We all seem to share a mental model of leadership and hierarchy in organizations that looks something like this:

I can’t draw…

It’s pervasive. It’s the common way we tend to think and talk about organizations. In practice, a real pyramid (from 1917) might look like:

By Tabulating Machine Co., December 1917; Picture by Marcin Wichary - File:Tabulating Machine Co org chart.mw.jpg, CC BY 2.5

The problem is, I think these pyramids are upside down. I think the pointy-end of the pyramid should be facing down, not up.

It’s a subtle distinction — but I think it’s important.

I feel like I’ve seen many would-be leaders, who seem to view leadership from the perspective of ‘moving up the ladder.’…they seem to think of it as gaining power, influence, freedom, …as being recognized (and rewarded) for contributions on ‘lower-rungs’ and rising above their peers, etc.

When people think of leadership as ‘moving up’ I think this mental model really hurts everyone, including the would-be leader. It gives them the wrong expectations, and when reality hits, it hurts.

⇢ I think reality looks a lot more like this:

In case my bad drawing is confusing — leaders have successively more people standing on their shoulders, as they take on larger leadership roles. It’s a pyramid, but it’s a reverse human pyramid. Leadership is heavy.

Leaders do not gain more power, they take on more responsibility. You don’t become a leader because you’re ‘the best among your peers’, and therefore your merit moves you up a pyramid.

You become a leader because you choose more responsibility for others, and move down the pyramid, with more weight on your shoulders.

You do not earn a ‘right to an opinion or a vote’ because you’re a leader. You have to work harder for your opinions, not less, as a leader. Because what you have is impact (for good or bad), not power. It’s meritocratic…≠ power. It’s ⇢ impact you have on the pyramid, not “power over”.

You don’t gain freedom. You gain impact, to (if you do a good job) positively help others. However you give up freedom, to take that responsibility, and make that impact.

From How and Why to Start A Startup - Sam Altman & Dustin Moskovitz

It’s interesting how much our language like “power over” reflects this bad mental-model of pyramids. It’s not flat-out wrong to describe organizational hierarchy. But it’s misleading, and so sets the wrong expectations.

So flip the pyramid upside down, to get a better mental-model. It may be less glamorous to think of, but it’s more real.

When you become a leader you will have people on your shoulders. Your behavior and actions (or lack thereof) could raise them up, or topple them down. You can have a great impact, but like Ben said, ‘with great power comes great responsibility’.

In the future I hope to do what I can to better-test leaders. I hope my expedition partners see pyramids with the pointy-end down.
