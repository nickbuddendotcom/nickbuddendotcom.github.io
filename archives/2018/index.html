Lastly, I try (but sometimes fail) to manage my time according to a philosophy of Live Well, which means being thoughtful about the number of hours I work each day to maintain my productivity over time.

More than a century of studies show that long-term useful worker output is maximized near a five-day, 40-hour workweek. Productivity drops immediately upon starting overtime and continues to drop until, at approximately eight 60-hour weeks, the total work done is the same as what would have been done in eight 40-hour weeks.

The above quote is from Why Crunch Modes Doesn‚Äôt Work: Six Lessons, a study of software engineers‚Äô productivity and total output by work-hours.

This reality has been shown to be true over and over again. We can work long hours and get more done in short bursts of 6-8 weeks at a time, but soon that increased productivity will evaporate or even reverse.

Despite this being proven true (repeatedly), some people still reject it, and we even experience guilt over it. Intuitively, it just feels wrong.

Why?

Well, from Peter Senge‚Äôs Fifth Discipline, on systems thinking:

THE DELUSION OF LEARNING FROM EXPERIENCE:

The most powerful learning comes from direct experience. Indeed, we learn eating, crawling, walking, and communicating through direct trial and error‚Äîthrough taking an action and seeing the consequences of that action; then taking a new and different action. But what happens when we can no longer observe the consequences of our actions? What happens if the primary consequences of our actions are in the distant future or in a distant part of the larger system within which we operate? We each have a ‚Äúlearning horizon,‚Äù a breadth of vision in time and space within which we assess our effectiveness. When our actions have consequences beyond our learning horizon, it becomes impossible to learn from direct experience.

So, what does this mean? Well, I‚Äôve spent 1-hour writing this blog post so far. If I extend that to 2-hours I‚Äôll get more done, right? Yes, I will.

That‚Äôs a simple, direct experience that tells me working a longer amount of time produces more work output. It‚Äôs where most people‚Äôs intuition comes from.

But the accumulated, eventual consequences of long work-hours summed over a long period of time is outside our learning horizon. So, we‚Äôre just not very good at intuiting it. Our spidey-sense often malfunctions when projected onto complex systems like this.

Anyway, I‚Äôm a workaholic by nature (just ask those who know me‚Ä¶). So it‚Äôs actually easier for me to work longer hours than it is to restrict myself. It comes naturally, while restricting myself does not.

However, alongside some other members of our team at Phase, I‚Äôve learned to become a lot better at this. It‚Äôs more productive (i.e. it works), because it‚Äôs not about working ‚Äòless hard‚Äô.

From Daniel Gross at his YC Startup School talk on How to Win:

I wanna be clear‚Ä¶I‚Äôm not proposing you don‚Äôt work hard. In order to win, you have to work hard. I‚Äôm proposing you don‚Äôt moronically work hard, and like, not sleep. Because then, you‚Äôre gonna have to work much harder, even, to catch up.

It‚Äôs about working with higher intensity, focus, and energy. That‚Äôs what restricting my work-hours each day to what‚Äôs been proven best, allows me to do. It actually helps me work harder.

For someone naturally inclined to working long hours (and enjoying it), restricting has eventually turned each day into a full-tilt, high-energy sprint of work. The sum of those daily, high-energy sprints is greater output than the previous long-hours marathons.

So today I encourage everyone to follow Hemingway‚Äôs advice, ‚ÄúThe important thing is to have good water in the well‚Ä¶and it is better to take a regular amount out than to pump the well dry and wait for it to refill.‚Äù

The overwhelming body of research is, well‚Ä¶correct (‚Ä¶duh (-_-)).

One final note is that despite everything I just said, I actually do still work long-hours, sometimes. I just try to be thoughtful about it, and exercise some control over when I make that short-term tradeoff.

I can give myself a short-term boost only a few times each year, so I want to use them wisely. It‚Äôs how I try to work both hard and smart.
